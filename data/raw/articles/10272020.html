Brightmark applies for tax incentives outside of Houston
US-based chemical plastics recycling start-up, Brightmark, has submitted a tax incentive application to construct a plant less than an hour northeast of Houston, TX.

Some context:
Brightmark recycles plastic by pyrolysis, which can convert the waste plastic into diesel, naphtha, or wax. The company currently operates just one plant in Indiana, but it's looking to build quite a few more as plastics recycling in the US takes off.

So what's this incentive application?
Inside the application, you'll find detailed plans for a potential site in Liberty County. If the application is accepted as is, Brightmark would spend $430 million on the project very soon.

Connecting some dots:
In case you hadn't heard, there are a lot of chemical plants near Houston, TX. Just a couple of weeks ago we saw CP Chem successfully use someone's pyrolysis oil to produce polyethylene. If Brightmark builds this plant they won't be short of interested customers.

Methanol from carbon emissions and water
Swiss-based energy, methanol, and fertilizer production company, Proman, has announced that it will be building the world's first green methanol plant in Belgium.

What are they doing?
The company will construct 2 plants: one hydrogen plant, and one methanol plant. The hydrogen plant will use renewable power to electrolyze water (splitting it into hydrogen and oxygen). The methanol plant will combine CO2 emissions (collected from other plants nearby) with that hydrogen to create methanol.

Bigger picture:
All of that methanol "can be used as a feedstock by the local chemical industry, and as a sustainable fuel for ships, road vehicles and trains.". The plants are being built in Belgium in response to the European Green Deal, and are prepared to expand production 10-fold in the future.

Neste's SAF finally enters the Asian market
Japan's largest airline, All Nippon Airways (ANA), has signed a supply agreement to use Neste's sustainable aviation fuel (SAF).

Have we seen this before?
In the last two month's we've seen Neste sign agreements with both Shell and BP. But that doesn't mean the company can't sign agreements with airlines as well.

So what's the deal?
ANA has agreed to "use SAF on flights departing from Japan and also represents Neste’s first SAF supply to an Asian airline". This is just the beginning because while right now Neste only produces 100,000 tons of SAF per year, they will be making 15 times as much by 2023.
